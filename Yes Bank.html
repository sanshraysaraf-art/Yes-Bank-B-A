Six dimensions of risk
P
Political
The US–Russia–
Gulf triangle

India navigates a triangular power dynamic where every relationship offers a discount but extracts a diplomatic cost.

  • Russia invasion (2022): crude share rose from 2% → 36% at a 35% discount — a windfall with a half-life
  • US threatened 25% tariffs, negotiated down to 18% via bilateral trade deal (early 2026)
  • Iran blocked Strait of Hormuz; India relied on Jaishankar's back-channels to maintain flow
  • BRICS currency frameworks explored to reduce structural petrodollar reliance
E
Economic
The impossible
macro trinity

Every $10/bbl rise adds $14–15B to the import bill — triggering a chain reaction no single policy lever can absorb.

  • Baseline import bill $150B; a 25% price spike widens it by $30–40B in a single year
  • CAD deteriorates → Rupee depreciates → forex reserves depleted — the classic oil shock spiral
  • Cannot simultaneously run expansionary monetary policy and control inflation
  • Contango market: near-term futures at $74/bbl; medium-term supply surplus expected
S
Social
1.4 billion people,
two vulnerabilities

Energy inflation hits hardest at the bottom. Gulf remittances create a second exposure that any Hormuz closure can sever overnight.

  • $51B in annual Gulf remittances — primary income for Kerala, UP, and Bihar households
  • LPG subsidy only viable below $80/bbl; above that, govt must raise prices or slash capex
  • Energy costs disproportionately burden lower-income households with no substitution options
  • Any Gulf employment disruption ripples through multiple states with no domestic buffer
T
Technological
Refineries locked
to heavy crude

Infrastructure built for Russian and Middle Eastern crude. The Strategic Petroleum Reserve sits critically short — by precisely 16 days of the IEA minimum.

  • Indian refineries optimised for heavy sour crude — cannot process US shale as a substitute
  • SPR at 74 days vs the IEA's 90-day minimum — a 16-day structural shortfall
  • BRICS digital currencies and shadow payment systems explored to bypass SWIFT
  • 100GW solar installed; targeting 500GW renewable capacity by 2030
L
Legal
G7 caps and
force majeure

Price caps and force majeure clauses rewrite the rules of oil trade — forcing India to improvise routes, currencies, and legal structures at scale.

  • G7 price cap at $60/bbl on Russian oil — Indian banks must route via non-dollar systems
  • War-risk insurance premiums spike when Hormuz is threatened, raising freight costs materially
  • Force majeure clauses push India from long-term contracts into volatile spot markets
  • Purchases rerouted through alternative shipping insurance to avoid Western legal exposure
E
Environmental
Demand rising as
pledges accumulate

Oil demand growing 3–4% annually even as India commits to net-zero by 2070 — a structural tension that colours every long-term energy decision.

  • India committed to net-zero by 2070 — among the later targets of any major economy
  • 100GW+ solar installed; targeting 500GW renewable capacity by 2030
  • Oil demand still rising 3–4% p.a. driven by industrialisation and transport growth
  • Core tension: energy security today vs decarbonisation credibility for the future

Why this matters beyond India's borders

India is now the world's third-largest oil importer. Its purchasing decisions move prices, its diplomatic positioning shapes alliances, and its balancing act between Washington and Moscow offers a preview of how other large emerging economies will navigate a fracturing world order.

The Russian crude arbitrage — buying discounted Urals while the West enforces price caps — is both a rational economic choice and a geopolitical statement. India's ability to sustain this position depends on BRICS solidarity, shadow payment resilience, and Washington's appetite for confrontation with a country it needs as a counterweight to China.

The 16-day gap in the Strategic Petroleum Reserve is not an administrative oversight. It reflects a deliberate trade-off: the fiscal cost of filling reserves against maintaining currency and fiscal headroom. That trade-off becomes catastrophic if Hormuz closes for more than three months.
